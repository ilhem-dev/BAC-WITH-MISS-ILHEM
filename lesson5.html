üìã Grammar Rules: Expressing Results with Intensity

Both "so ... that" and "such ... that" express results and consequences.

We use them to connect two sentences where the first causes the second due to intensity or degree.

üìè SO ... THAT Structure

Subject + verb + SO + adjective + THAT + Subject + verb + object

Example:

The government was so corrupted that it caused economic collapse.

Note: "so" comes before adjectives and adverbs

üè∑Ô∏è SUCH ... THAT Structure

Subject + verb + SUCH + noun phrase + THAT + Subject + verb + object

Example:

Russia had such a corrupted government that it caused economic collapse.

Note: "such" comes before noun phrases (a/an + adjective + noun)

üîë Key Differences:

  • SO + adjective/adverb + THAT
  • SUCH + (a/an) + adjective + noun + THAT
  • Both express the same meaning with different grammar structures

Item 1

Combine: Sannah's feet are big. Sannah can't find shoes that could be her size.

Item 2

Combine: Our teacher speaks clearly. Everyone can understand the teacher.

Item 3

Combine: They had a bad night. They couldn't sleep.

Item 4

Combine: It was cold weather. Nobody could go outside.

Item 5

Combine: It was dark. I couldn't see her face.

Item 6

Combine: The day was hot. Everybody went to the beach.

Item 7

Combine: Most TV programs are boring. Nobody watches them.
